The camera does not see your sherwani the way your eye does. Under a videographer's lights, warm halls push red, LED panels flatten everything, and daylight is brutally honest about cheap fabric. I have stood beside enough wedding shoots in Rawalpindi and Islamabad to know which colours survive the album and which ones grooms regret when they see the film. Here is how to choose for the camera, not just the mirror.

## How light changes colour on camera

Three lighting situations cover almost every Pakistani shaadi. Each treats colour differently.

| Light | What it does | Colours that win |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Outdoor daylight | Honest, cool, shows texture | Ivory, sand, bottle green, navy |
| Warm hall / banquet | Pushes red and yellow | Oxblood, navy, black with gold |
| LED / video panels | Flattens, cools, sharpens | Bottle green, navy, raw-silk blacks |

Pale colours blow out in bright sun and lose their detail. Very dark colours close up in dim halls and your embroidery disappears. The trick is matching your colour to where the headline photos will actually be taken.

## The colours that always read well

If I had to bet on what photographs cleanest across most setups, I would name three.

- **Midnight navy** — reads dark and formal but never closes up the way black can. The most camera-friendly dark on the shelf.
- **Bottle green** — holds its richness in every light and gives the colourist something warm to work with.
- **Ivory with tilla** — for day functions and walima, glows softly without blowing out if the fabric is good.

## Why fabric matters more than colour on camera

This is the part grooms underestimate. A flat polyester catches light as a dead patch. A woven jamawar or a deep velvet catches light across its surface, so even a plain colour photographs with depth. The camera loves texture.

- **Velvet** photographs richest of all, especially in dark colours under warm light. It absorbs and glows.
- **Jamawar** gives self-texture that keeps a solid colour from looking flat in wide shots.
- **Raw silk** reads sharp and structured, good for clean modern frames.

## Contrast with the bridal joora

The single most common photography mistake: the dulha and dulhan both in heavily worked, similarly bright outfits. In the frame they merge into one busy mass. The eye needs a quiet partner.

A few simple rules we give every groom:

1. **If she is in heavy red or maroon,** a black, navy or bottle green sherwani frames her beautifully without competing.
2. **If her outfit is pastel or ivory,** you can go richer and darker so you anchor the photo.
3. **Avoid both of you in gold-heavy outfits** under warm light; the photo turns into one golden blur.
4. **Share a swatch before you stitch.** A two-minute exchange saves a thousand awkward frames.

## Colour by function, for the album

Most grooms are photographed across three nights. Plan colour so the album has variety, not three near-identical spreads.

- **Mehndi:** lighter and warmer plays well in the typically brighter, festive lighting. Mustard, pista, ivory.
- **Barat:** the dramatic frame. Black, oxblood or bottle green under hall lights.
- **Walima:** refined and lighter again. Ivory or soft navy so the album closes calm, not loud.

## Small things that ruin photos

After years of this I keep a mental list of what tanks a groom's wedding photos, regardless of colour.

- A **flat, cheap inner kurta** that goes grey under light. Always crisp white or ivory.
- **Pale powder pastels** on the barat; they wash to nothing on camera.
- **Over-stuffed embroidery** everywhere, which reads as visual noise in wide shots.
- A **poor fit** across the shoulders, which the camera exaggerates mercilessly. Fit first; our [measurement guide](/measurement-guide) explains why.
