Grooms spend weeks on the sherwani fabric and the embroidery, then decide the bottoms in thirty seconds at the counter. That is a mistake. What you wear below the sherwani changes the entire silhouette, the formality, and how your build reads in photos. In Saddar we cut all three, churidar, trouser and shalwar, and each has its place. Let me settle the debate properly.

## The three options, honestly compared

| Bottom | Look | Formality | Best for |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Churidar | Traditional, fitted ankle, regal | Highest | Barat, classic grooms |
| Straight trouser | Clean, modern, sharp | High | Walima, prince coat, evening |
| Shalwar | Relaxed, traditional, roomy | Moderate | Mehndi, smaller functions |

There is no single right answer. There is a right answer for your function, your build and the silhouette you want. Below, the detail.

## Churidar: the classic barat choice

The churidar is the traditional partner to a sherwani, and for the main barat it is still my first recommendation for most grooms. Cut tight from knee to ankle, it gathers in soft folds, the chooris, at the ankle.

- **Pros:** the most formal and traditional line, finishes the sherwani the way it was designed to be worn, photographs regal.
- **Cons:** demands a good fit and a slim-to-average lower leg to look its best; a badly cut churidar bags and ruins the line.
- **Footwear:** khussa or a clean Peshawari, never a Western shoe.

A churidar pairs especially well with a longer, traditional sherwani in velvet or jamawar. If you are choosing your barat look around a dark base, our [black sherwani guide](/blog/black-sherwani-guide) covers how the churidar completes it.

## Straight trouser: the modern, clean choice

A tailored straight trouser, cut like good suit trousering in a matching tone, reads contemporary and sharp. It has quietly become the default for grooms who want a cleaner, more European line.

- **Pros:** clean vertical line, easy to wear and walk in, flatters almost every build, especially heavier ones.
- **Cons:** slightly less traditional; can look plain if the fabric or break is wrong.
- **Best with:** a shorter, structured sherwani or a [prince coat](/collections/prince-coat), and for the walima above all.

For a prince coat the straight trouser is almost always the better choice; the silhouette is built around it. If you are still deciding between the two jackets, our [sherwani versus prince coat](/blog/sherwani-vs-prince-coat) comparison is the place to start.

## Shalwar: relaxed and traditional

A shalwar with a sherwani is the most relaxed of the three. Roomy, comfortable, deeply traditional, it suits the mehndi and smaller family functions far better than the main barat.

- **Pros:** comfortable through a long function, breathable in warmer weather, authentically desi.
- **Cons:** the least formal line; can read as casual under a heavily worked barat sherwani.
- **Best for:** mehndi, dholki, nikah at home, where ease matters more than maximum formality.

If you are mapping outfits across the whole shaadi, our [barat, walima and mehndi groom dress guide](/blog/barat-walima-mehndi-groom-dress-guide) shows where each bottom belongs.

## Choosing by build

Your lower body should guide the choice as much as the function.

- **Slim legs:** churidar looks excellent; you can carry the fitted ankle line.
- **Heavier build:** straight trouser keeps a clean vertical and avoids drawing the eye to the calf. Our [groom fit guide by body type](/blog/groom-fit-guide-by-body-type) explains why the vertical line matters so much.
- **Short groom:** churidar or a slim straight trouser both lengthen the leg; avoid anything that bunches at the ankle and shortens you.
- **Tall groom:** all three work; a churidar adds the most traditional drama.

## Fit is everything below the sherwani too

A churidar lives and dies by its fit. Too loose and it bags at the knee; too tight and you cannot sit through the rukhsati. The folds at the ankle should be deliberate, three or four clean chooris, not a crumpled mess. We take a separate naap for the bottoms for exactly this reason, which is part of every proper [measurement](/measurement-guide) session.

A few rules we hold to:

1. The churidar ankle should gather in clean folds, never sag.
2. A straight trouser should have a slight break over the shoe, not pool around it.
3. The bottom colour should match or sit one tone darker than the sherwani, never lighter.
4. Always match your footwear to the bottom before the function, not on the day.

## My honest recommendation

For a traditional barat in a longer sherwani, wear a churidar; nothing else finishes the look as well. For a walima or any prince-coat outfit, wear a straight trouser. For the mehndi, a shalwar keeps you comfortable through hours of dancing. Match the bottom to the moment and the whole outfit lifts.
