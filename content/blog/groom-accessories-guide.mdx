A sherwani is only half the dulha. The other half is everything around it — the pagri on the head, the khussa on the feet, the mala at the chest, the brooch catching the light. I have watched grooms spend a fortune on the sherwani and then ruin the photographs with a rented turban that does not fit and plastic shoes. The accessories are not an afterthought; they are what turn a fine outfit into a complete groom. Here is how we coordinate them in our Saddar shop.

## The kulla: the foundation of the headpiece

The kulla is the rigid conical cap that sits under the pagri and gives it height and shape. Without a proper kulla, even an expensive pagri slumps and looks soft. Kullas come in different heights and finishes — plain, gota-edged, or worked with tilla and stones for the barat.

The rule I give every groom: the kulla sets the formality. A taller, worked kulla reads as a full barat statement; a shorter plain one keeps things restrained for a walima or mehndi. Choose the kulla first, then build the pagri on it.

## The pagri: the crown

The pagri (the wound turban cloth) is the single most important accessory, because it is at eye level in every photo. There are several regional styles — the Punjabi shaadi pagri, the Sindhi style, the more structured ready-tied options — and the right one depends on your face, your height, and your sherwani. We cover the styles in detail in our [pagri and kulla styles](/blog/pagri-and-kulla-styles) piece, but the headline advice is this:

- A tall groom can carry a fuller, higher pagri. A shorter groom should keep it neat and not too wide, or it overwhelms the face.
- A ready-tied pagri on a kulla is the most reliable for a long day — it will not loosen during the barat.
- The shamla (the fanned tail at the back) should sit clean and even. A drooping shamla ruins the whole effect.

Get the pagri tied and checked the day before, not in the chaos of the morning.

## The brooch and kalgi

The kalgi is the ornament — historically a plume, today a jewelled brooch and feather — pinned to the front of the pagri. It is the focal jewel of the headpiece. Keep it proportionate: a heavy worked pagri wants a substantial kalgi, while a plain pagri is lifted by a single elegant brooch. Match the metal tone (gold, antique, or silver) to the dominant tone in your sherwani embroidery, and you cannot go far wrong.

## The khussa and footwear

Your feet are in more photos than you think — the joota chupai alone guarantees it. The khussa is the most traditional choice and balances a heavily embroidered sherwani beautifully, especially worked or zari khussa for the barat. For lighter or more modern looks, a clean Peshawari chappal or a dark Oxford works. We go deep on this in [wedding shoes for the groom](/blog/wedding-shoes-for-the-groom), but the short version:

| Shoe | Best with | Note |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Worked khussa | Heavy barat sherwani | Most traditional, ties the look together |
| Plain khussa | Walima, mehndi | Understated, comfortable |
| Peshawari chappal | Casual barat, mehndi | Relaxed but smart |
| Dark Oxford | Prince coat, modern look | Sharp, Western-leaning |

Whatever you pick, break the shoes in for a few days before the wedding. Brand-new khussa over a six-hour barat is its own kind of suffering.

## The mala and chest pieces

The sehra and the mala (the flower or beaded garlands) are worn by the dulha at the barat. Fresh-flower malas are traditional and beautiful but heavy and short-lived; beaded or pearl malas last the whole event and photograph consistently. If your sherwani already carries heavy embroidery at the collar and chest, go lighter on the mala so the two do not fight. A worked collar plus a heavy mala is too much at the neckline.

## Buttons, shawls, and the small things

Do not overlook the smallest details. Kundan or worked buttons lift a plain sherwani instantly. A fine pashmina or worked shawl draped over the shoulders adds drama for a cold winter barat and keeps you warm between photos. A pocket square or a brooch at the chest can echo the kalgi. These small touches are cheap relative to the sherwani and they finish the look.

## Coordinating it all

The principle is simple: one hero, everything else in support. Usually the sherwani embroidery is the hero, so the accessories should pick up its tones rather than compete. Choose two metal tones at most across the whole look. Keep the colour palette tight. When in doubt, calmer wins — the camera and the guests should see a complete, intentional groom, not a pile of separate expensive things.
