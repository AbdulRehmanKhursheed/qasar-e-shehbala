The nikah is the quiet one. There is no dhol, no stage entrance, often just family and close friends in a drawing room or a masjid, and a maulvi sahib doing the actual work of the day. Grooms walk into our shop on Saddar Road convinced they need to be in full ceremonial dress for it, and most of the time they do not. The nikah rewards restraint. Get the fit right, keep the colour soft, and you will look better in those close, candid photographs than the man drowning in dabka.

Here is how we dress a groom for his nikah.

## Read the room first

Before you choose anything, ask one question: how big is the nikah, and where is it? The answer decides everything.

- **Home or masjid nikah, immediate family only.** Keep it simple and refined. A fine kurta or a waistcoat over a kurta is plenty.
- **Nikah combined with a small dinner or engagement.** Step it up to a prince coat. You want a little more structure for a seated, formal evening.
- **Nikah on the same day as the barat.** Many grooms now wear one strong look for both. If that is you, lean toward the barat and read our [sherwani vs prince coat guide](/blog/sherwani-vs-prince-coat) before deciding.

The mistake is dressing for the wedding you saw on Instagram instead of the rasm actually happening in front of you.

## Option one: the fine kurta

The simplest answer, and often the best. A well-cut kurta in raw silk, fine cotton, or a soft khaddar reads clean and dignified. Pair it with a chooridar pajama or a straight trouser, and a good khussa.

- Choose ivory, off-white, or a muted tone. The nikah is not the function for bright colour.
- Spend on the cloth, not surface work. A heavy silk kurta with a self-tone collar looks more expensive than a cheap fabric loaded with gota.
- Keep the embroidery to the collar and placket only, if any at all.

A kurta lives in your wardrobe long after the shaadi. You will wear it on Eid, to other people's nikahs, to dinners. That re-wearability is the quiet bonus.

## Option two: waistcoat over a kurta

This is our favourite nikah look, and the one we recommend most. A structured waistcoat over a plain kurta gives you shape and seriousness without the weight of a full coat. It photographs beautifully because the waistcoat frames the chest and shoulders.

- A bandhgala-collar waistcoat in a deep tone over an ivory kurta is a combination that never looks wrong.
- Velvet waistcoats work well for a winter nikah; a textured suiting fabric suits the warmer months.
- Add a pocket square and a slim brooch if the family theme calls for a little detail.

Browse the [waistcoat collection](/collections/waistcoats) to see the cuts and collar shapes — the difference between a flat, sad waistcoat and a sharp one is entirely in the naap and the canvas inside it.

## Option three: the prince coat

If your nikah is a proper seated evening, a [prince coat](/collections/prince-coat) is the grown-up choice. It is sharp, semi-formal, and it does not compete with the bride or with your own barat sherwani the next day. Navy, charcoal, bottle green, and stone all work.

The beauty of choosing a prince coat for the nikah is that it is genuinely re-wearable. Every friend's shaadi for the next five years, you have a coat ready. A barat sherwani cannot say that.

## What not to do

A few things we gently talk grooms out of, every season:

- **Do not over-embellish.** Heavy zardozi at a small nikah looks like you wandered in from the wrong function.
- **Do not match the bride exactly.** Complement her tone, do not copy it. Share a swatch between you beforehand.
- **Do not wear black tie or a Western three-piece** unless the whole event is styled that way. It sits oddly against a traditional rasm.
- **Do not leave the fit to chance.** A loose collar or a baggy waist ruins even the finest fabric.
