The colour you choose is the first thing a guest registers from across the hall, long before they notice the dabka work on your collar. I have cut sherwanis in Saddar since the late nineties, and every season a few colours rise and a few quietly retire. For the 2026 wedding crowd, five shades are doing most of the work. Here is how they actually behave on a real dulha, under real shaadi lighting, on real skin.

## The five colours that matter in 2026

People walk in asking for "something different," then point at the same five swatches. That is fine. These shades earned their place.

- **Black** — the safe king. Sharp, formal, photographs clean.
- **Bottle green** — the breakout of the last two seasons, rich without shouting.
- **Oxblood** — deep maroon-brown, warm and a little old-money.
- **Midnight navy** — black's quieter cousin, softer on most faces.
- **Ivory and gold** — the day-function and walima favourite.

If you want the full case for going dark, I have written a separate piece on [the black sherwani](/blog/black-sherwani-guide). Here I want to compare across the whole shelf.

## Matching the colour to your skin tone

This is where most grooms go wrong. They pick a colour they like on a mannequin, not on themselves. Naap aur rang dono apni shakl ke hisaab se hone chahiye.

| Skin tone | Flatters | Be careful with |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Fair / pinkish | Bottle green, oxblood, navy | Pure ivory can wash you out |
| Wheatish (most common here) | Almost everything, especially black and green | Very pale beige |
| Deep / dark | Ivory, gold, navy, bottle green | Flat charcoal under harsh halls |

A wheatish complexion, which is what most of our Rawalpindi and Islamabad grooms have, is the easiest to dress. You can wear black confidently. But if your skin runs deep, do not let anyone push you into a dull grey-black. Go for ivory with tilla, or a bottle green that puts warmth back into the photos.

## Venue and lighting change everything

A colour that looks regal in our shop's daylight can turn muddy under a banquet hall's yellow LEDs.

- **Outdoor day barat or mehndi:** lighter shades win. Ivory, sand, pista green. Heavy black absorbs sunlight and you will sweat through your jamawar by the second hour.
- **Indoor evening halls:** deep tones come alive. Oxblood, navy and bottle green catch the warm light and look expensive. This is also where black truly belongs.
- **Mixed marquee functions:** navy is the diplomat. It reads dark indoors and softens outdoors without ever looking wrong.

For grooms shooting a full cinematic film, I would also point you to our notes on [sherwani colours for wedding photography](/blog/sherwani-colours-for-wedding-photography), because the camera does not see colour the way your eye does.

## Colour by function across the shaadi

Most grooms now need two or three outfits across mehndi, barat and walima. Repeating the same colour in slightly different tones looks lazy. Plan a small journey instead.

1. **Mehndi:** play. Mustard, pista, soft teal, ivory with colour-thread work. This is the one night to be a little loud.
2. **Barat:** the heavyweight. Black, oxblood or bottle green, fully worked. This is your headline outfit.
3. **Walima:** refined and lighter. Ivory, beige-gold or a soft navy prince coat reads beautifully here.

If you are building a coordinated set across all three nights, the colour story matters as much as the cut. Our [barat, walima and mehndi groom dress guide](/blog/barat-walima-mehndi-groom-dress-guide) maps the whole sequence.

## The qeemat reality

Colour itself does not change the price much; the fabric and embroidery do. But certain colours are usually chosen with heavier work, so they trend pricier in practice.

- A clean **black or navy** sherwani in good jamawar with tonal work: roughly PKR 65,000 to 120,000 in our made-to-measure range.
- An **oxblood or bottle green** with heavier dabka and zardozi: often PKR 110,000 to 180,000, because grooms rarely keep these plain.
- **Ivory with tilla** for walima: PKR 55,000 to 130,000, depending on how dense the gold work runs.

These are honest ranges for our karigari, not import markups. The exact qeemat depends on your fabric and how loud you want the embroidery.

## A few opinions, freely given

After all these years I will say it plainly. Black is never a mistake, but it is rarely memorable on its own; let your embroidery or your buttons do the talking. Bottle green is the smartest choice for a groom who wants to look current without gambling. Oxblood is for the confident man with a warm complexion. And ivory belongs on the walima far more than on the barat, where it gets lost against a heavily dressed bride.

Skip pastel pinks and powder blues for the main barat. They photograph weak and they age badly in the album five years later. Save soft colours for the mehndi.
