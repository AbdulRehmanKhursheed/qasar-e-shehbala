Grooms walk in worried about their body. Too tall, too short, too heavy, too thin. I tell every one of them the same thing first: there is no bad body type, only a bad fit. A correctly cut sherwani flatters every build, and a sloppy one ruins the best jamawar on the finest physique. That said, each build does have a few rules that make it look its best. Here is what I have learned cutting for every kind of dulha in Rawalpindi over the years.

## Fit beats body type, every time

Before we talk builds, understand what fit actually means. It is not "tight." A sherwani that grips the body looks cheap and moves badly. Fit means the garment sits clean on the shoulders, skims the torso, and falls in a straight vertical line.

The three points that decide everything:

1. **Shoulder seam** lands exactly at the edge of your shoulder, not before, not hanging over.
2. **Chest and waist** skim with a hand's width of ease, never pull or balloon.
3. **Length and line** stay vertical and uninterrupted from collar to hem.

Get a proper naap and these are correct by default. If you want to understand how a real measurement session works, our [how to take sherwani measurements](/blog/how-to-take-sherwani-measurements) guide and the [measurement guide](/measurement-guide) explain it step by step.

## The tall, slim groom

The easiest build to dress, and the most likely to be over-styled into looking like a flagpole.

- **Length:** you can carry a longer sherwani, to mid-calf, without losing proportion.
- **Structure:** a touch of padding and a fuller chest stops you looking narrow.
- **Fabric:** velvet and jamawar add welcome weight and richness. Avoid thin, flat fabrics that emphasise the lean line.
- **Embroidery:** horizontal motifs across the chest add breadth. All-over vertical work makes you look even taller.
- **Avoid:** very high button stances and skinny churidars that exaggerate the length.

## The shorter groom

The goal is one long vertical line, uninterrupted, so the eye reads height.

- **Length:** stop just above the knee. Ankle-length cuts the body and shortens you.
- **Button stance:** slightly raised, which lifts the waistline and lengthens the legs.
- **Fabric:** medium-weight, structured. Heavy velvet can overwhelm a smaller frame.
- **Embroidery:** vertical placket and collar work draws the eye up.
- **Bottoms:** a churidar gives a clean ankle line and adds visual height. See [churidar or trousers with your sherwani](/blog/churidar-or-trousers-with-sherwani).
- **Avoid:** wide contrast borders at the hem, which chop your height in half.

## The heavier groom

Black and navy were practically invented for this build. The aim is a clean, slimming vertical with structure doing the work.

- **Colour:** dark. Black, midnight navy, oxblood. Our [black sherwani guide](/blog/black-sherwani-guide) explains why black slims so well.
- **Cut:** single-breasted, structured shoulder, a length to mid-thigh that skims rather than clings.
- **Fabric:** matte and structured. Avoid shiny or clingy fabrics that catch on the body.
- **Embroidery:** concentrated at the collar and placket, not all over. Heavy all-over work adds bulk.
- **Inner kurta:** crisp, not bunched, so there is no extra mass under the front.
- **Avoid:** light colours, big shiny buttons, and tight cuts that pull across the chest.

## The slim, average-height groom

The most common build we cut, and the most flexible.

- **Structure:** a little shoulder structure and chest fullness gives presence.
- **Fabric:** jamawar is ideal, with enough body to fill the frame without bulk.
- **Cut:** a clean tailored fit, skimming, never skin-tight. A sherwani is not a suit jacket.
- **Embroidery:** you can wear most styles; chest-width motifs add the most presence.

## Quick reference

| Build | Length | Best colour | Fabric | Watch out for |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Tall / slim | Mid-calf | Rich jewel tones | Velvet, jamawar | Looking too narrow |
| Short | Above knee | Mid tones, vertical work | Medium structured | Hem borders, ankle length |
| Heavier | Mid-thigh | Black, navy, oxblood | Matte structured | All-over heavy work |
| Slim average | Knee | Almost anything | Jamawar | Cutting it too tight |

## The thing nobody tells you

Posture changes the fit more than two kilos either way. A groom who stands tall in a well-cut sherwani always looks better than one who slouches in a fortune of velvet. We cut to how you carry yourself, which is why the trial fitting matters so much. Never accept a sherwani that "will be fine once it's pressed." If it does not sit right at the trial, it never will.
