People walk into our shop on Saddar Road and ask the same question almost every week: *"Sherwani lun ya prince coat?"* It is the right question to ask, and the honest answer is that it depends on three things — the function, your body, and how you want to feel walking in.

After stitching groom wear since 1999, here is how we actually advise grooms, without the showroom sales talk.

## The short version

A **sherwani** is the ceremonial, traditional choice. Long, regal, usually embellished, worn with a kulla or pagri and khussa. It is what most people picture when they think *dulha*.

A **prince coat** is sharper and more European in its lines. Knee-length or just above, fitted through the waist, often plainer. It reads as confident and modern rather than ceremonial.

Neither is "better." They are built for different moments.

## Match the garment to the function

This is the part most grooms get wrong. They pick a garment they like in a photo, then realise it does not suit the event.

- **Barat** — This is the big entrance, the dhol, the stage, hundreds of photos. Go sherwani. The volume and the embroidery earn their keep here, and a kulla finishes the look. Our [sherwani collection](/collections/sherwani) is built for exactly this.
- **Walima** — A more formal, dinner-style evening. Either works, but a [prince coat](/collections/prince-coat) in a deep navy, bottle green, or charcoal looks effortless and lets your bride's outfit lead. Many grooms wear a sherwani to the barat and a prince coat to the walima — a smart split if the budget allows.
- **Nikah / engagement** — A prince coat or a fine [waistcoat](/collections/waistcoats) over a kurta is plenty. A full embellished sherwani at a small nikah can feel like too much.

## Body type — what actually flatters

Forget the rules you read online. Here is what we see on real grooms in the fitting room:

- **Tall and lean** — You can carry almost anything. A floor-skimming sherwani with a strong border looks excellent on you.
- **Shorter build** — Keep the sherwani length just below the knee and skip the heavy hem border, which visually cuts your height. A prince coat is genuinely a great option here — its cleaner line elongates you.
- **Heavier build** — A structured shoulder, a darker matte fabric, and a fit that skims rather than clings. Avoid shiny fabrics and busy all-over embroidery, which add bulk. A semi-fitted prince coat in charcoal is very forgiving.

The single biggest factor is not your build — it is the **naap**. A perfectly fitted sherwani on a heavier groom beats a poorly fitted one on a lean groom every time.

## Fabric and embellishment

Sherwanis are usually made in jamawar, raw silk, or velvet, and carry the handwork — dabka, tilla, zardozi, resham. Prince coats lean on structure rather than surface decoration, so a good wool-blend or a textured suiting fabric does most of the work.

If you want to understand which fabric suits the winter wedding season, read our [guide to sherwani fabrics](/blog/jamawar-velvet-banarsi-fabric-guide) — it covers jamawar, velvet, and banarsi in detail.

## What about the qeemat?

Roughly speaking:

| Garment | Typical made-to-measure range (PKR) |
| --- | --- |
| Plain prince coat (wool-blend) | 35,000 – 70,000 |
| Embellished prince coat | 70,000 – 130,000 |
| Sherwani (moderate handwork) | 80,000 – 180,000 |
| Heavily embellished sherwani | 180,000 and above |

These are honest ranges for quality made-to-measure work, not mass-produced showroom pieces. The biggest cost driver is always the handwork, not the fabric.

## Our advice

If you can only choose one garment and the barat is the main event, choose the **sherwani**. It is the photograph you will keep. If your walima is the bigger function, or you want something you can genuinely wear again to a friend's wedding, the **prince coat** earns its place.

And whichever you choose — give us time. A proper sherwani is cut to your body and takes 10 to 15 working days. Anyone promising a custom one in three days is selling you a ready-made piece with your name on the bill.
