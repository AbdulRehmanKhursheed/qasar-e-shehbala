Not every groom needs to own a sherwani, and I will say that openly even though we are in the business of stitching them. Some functions genuinely call for a rental. The trouble is that grooms often rent for the wrong occasion and buy for the wrong one too. After dressing dulhas in Saddar since 1999, here is the honest comparison, with no thumb on the scale.

## The case for renting

Renting is straightforward. You pick from what is on the rack, the shop adjusts it lightly, you wear it once, you return it. For the right occasion it is a smart, light-on-the-wallet choice.

Where renting earns its place:

- **You are a guest, not the dulha.** A friend's mehndi or a cousin's barat where you just want to look sharp for one evening.
- **A one-time, low-stakes function.** An engagement dinner you will not repeat.
- **You want a look you would never wear again.** A bold colour or very heavy piece you would not buy.
- **Tight budget, tight timeline.** No room to stitch, limited funds, a single event.

## The case for buying

Buying, especially made-to-measure, is the choice when the day matters and the garment will be seen up close and photographed for the rest of your life.

Where buying wins:

- **It is your own shaadi.** Your barat and walima are not the place for a rack-fit sherwani.
- **You want it cut for your body.** Shoulders, chest balance and collar that actually sit, which a rental cannot promise.
- **You will wear it more than once.** Barat, walima, a sibling's wedding next year, family functions.
- **Sentiment.** Many grooms keep their barat sherwani. You cannot keep a rental.

## Cost: it depends entirely on how many days

Grooms see the rental sticker, compare it to the stitching qeemat, and assume renting wins. Sometimes. Let us be honest about the full picture.

| Factor | Renting | Buying (made-to-measure) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Upfront cost | Lower per event | Higher, one time |
| Security deposit | Yes, often sizeable | None |
| Cost for two functions (barat plus walima) | Two rental fees, adds up fast | One garment, reused |
| Fit | Standard, lightly altered | Cut to your body |
| Keep it after | No | Yes |
| Damage liability | You pay for stains and tears | It is yours |

For one event, renting is cheaper, no argument. But the dulha rarely has one event. Once you are paying two rental fees plus deposits for barat and walima, the gap to a bought sherwani narrows sharply, and the bought one fits better and stays with you. ## Fit: the quiet weakness of rentals

A rental is cut to a standard size and altered just enough to pass between customers. The shoulders and collar, the hardest areas to fit, are set by whoever the garment was originally built for, not you. So a rented sherwani often pulls across the back or gapes at the neck, and the alterations a rental shop will do are limited because they need to undo them for the next groom.

This is exactly where made-to-measure pulls ahead, and it is why we never recommend renting for the dulha's own functions. For the detail on what a proper fit involves, see our [made-to-measure versus readymade comparison](/blog/made-to-measure-vs-readymade-sherwani).

## Hygiene: worth thinking about honestly

A rented sherwani has been worn by many men through long, warm functions. Good shops dry-clean between wears, and most are responsible about it. But heavy embroidery and inner linings do not always come fully fresh from a quick clean, and a barat in summer means a lot of sweat. If wearing something many bodies have worn through your own wedding bothers you, that is a legitimate reason to buy. Some grooms do not mind at all. Only you can weigh that.

## Sentiment: small but real

This one is easy to dismiss until years later. Plenty of men keep their barat sherwani, take it out for an anniversary photo, or pass the idea of it to a younger brother. A rental goes back the next morning. It is not a financial argument, but for many families the sherwani is part of the memory of the day, and you cannot keep what you rented.

## Our honest verdict

Here is how we actually advise grooms:

1. **Guest at someone else's function, one evening, tight budget?** Rent. No shame, sensible choice.
2. **Your own barat or walima?** Buy, and buy made-to-measure if your timeline allows.
3. **Two of your own functions in different looks?** Buy one well and consider renting or a simpler second piece for the lighter event, like mehndi.

One mild contrarian take: a lot of grooms rent a heavily embroidered showpiece thinking heavier means grander, then spend the night in a sherwani that does not fit and is not theirs. A modest, well-fitted bought sherwani beats a borrowed heavy one every time, in person and in photos.
