Grooms in Islamabad often assume they need to find a tailor in F-10 or Blue Area, and grooms in Rawalpindi sometimes drive past the one district that actually does this work best. If you want a prince coat cut properly — fitted through the waist, shaped at the shoulder, finished by a karigar who makes them every week — Saddar is where you go. It has been the groom-wear district for decades, and the twin cities are a short drive apart. This is a practical local guide: lead times, deposits, what to ask, and how not to get burned.

## Why Saddar, and not closer to home

A prince coat is a structured garment. The shoulder needs canvas, the waist needs shaping, and the collar has to close clean. That comes from karigars who cut coats every day, not from a general tailor who mostly does shalwar-kameez and takes a coat now and then.

- **Concentration of skill.** Saddar Road and the surrounding lanes have the master tailors and the embroidery karigars in one place.
- **Fabric on hand.** You can see and feel wool-blends, suiting, and velvet in person rather than ordering blind.
- **Fittings are easy.** Both Pindi and Islamabad grooms can reach Saddar for a mid-stitch fitting without a real journey.

It is a twenty to forty minute drive from most of Islamabad. For a coat you will wear in your wedding photographs, that is nothing.

## Lead times — plan backwards from the wedding

This is the number grooms get wrong most often. A prince coat is not a same-week garment if you want it cut to your body.

| Type of coat | Working days needed |
| --- | --- |
| Plain wool-blend prince coat | 10 to 15 |
| Coat with light embroidery | 15 to 20 |
| Heavily embellished coat | 20 to 30 |

Now layer the season on top. The wedding season runs Oct–Feb, and the Saddar workrooms fill up from August onward as grooms place orders. If your shaadi is in December, you want to be choosing fabric in September, not the first week of December. The grooms who walk in ten days before the wedding are the ones who end up settling for whatever is on the rack.

## What to ask the tailor

A good tailor will happily answer all of this. If the answers are vague, walk on.

1. **"Is this fully made to measure, or made-to-order from a block?"** You want your own naap, not a standard block resized.
2. **"How many fittings, and when?"** A proper coat gets at least one mid-stitch fitting. One measurement and then collection is a warning sign.
3. **"Can I see the fabric and the canvas?"** The internal canvas is what holds a coat's shape. Ask to see it.
4. **"What is the lead time, honestly?"** Anyone promising a custom coat in three days is selling you a ready-made piece.
5. **"What is the deposit and the balance schedule?"** See below.
6. **"What if the fit is off at final fitting?"** A reputable house will alter it. Get that confirmed before you pay.

## Deposits — what is normal

Deposits are standard and reasonable. The tailor is buying fabric and committing a karigar's time on your order.

- A deposit of roughly **half the total** at the time of order is normal practice.
- The **balance** is due at the final fitting or collection.
- **Get a receipt** with the amount, the agreed delivery date, and the garment details written on it.

Be cautious at both extremes. A tailor who takes nothing upfront has no commitment to your timeline, and one who demands the full amount with no fitting plan has no incentive to get the fit right.
