People walk into our Saddar workshop and point at a sherwani and say "I want this much work." Then they touch the chest panel and ask why one costs double the other when both look busy from a distance. The answer is almost always in the embroidery: what thread, whose hands, how dense, and where it sits. After twenty-five years of putting a dulha into barat clothes, I can tell you the embroidery is where the qeemat lives and where most mistakes get made.

Let me walk you through the three names everyone uses loosely, because understanding them is the difference between paying for craft and paying for noise.

## Dabka: the coiled wire that catches light

Dabka is a thin metallic wire, tightly coiled like a spring, then cut into tiny lengths and stitched down by hand. Because it is hollow and coiled, it grabs light from every angle. Run your finger across good dabka and you feel a soft, raised ridge, not a flat printed shine.

On a sherwani, dabka is what gives the collar and the front placket that dense, jewel-like glow under indoor shaadi lighting. It comes in matte and shiny finishes, and in gold, silver, antique (oxidised), and rose tones. The karigar lays it in rows, in florals, in geometric jaali — and the tighter and more even the rows, the more skilled the hand.

Dabka is rarely used alone. It is the workhorse material inside a larger zardozi design.

## Zardozi: the full vocabulary of heavy work

When people say "zardozi sherwani," they usually mean the whole composition — the raised metal embroidery that combines dabka, naqshi (small metal beads), sitara (sequins), kora, salma, and often kundan or semi-precious stones. Zardozi is not one stitch; it is a craft tradition. A good zardozi karigar builds a motif in layers, padding the base with cotton thread so the design sits proud of the fabric.

This is the work that reads as "heavy" and "royal." A maroon velvet barat sherwani with a fully worked yoke, a worked placket running to the hem, and worked cuffs is almost always zardozi. It is also the most labour-intensive and the most expensive, because one karigar may spend two to three weeks on a single piece stretched on the adda frame.

## Tilla: the thread that whispers instead of shouts

Tilla is metallic thread — historically real gold or silver wrapped over a silk core, today usually a fine gilt thread — worked as embroidery rather than as coiled wire. Tilla work has a flatter, more woven look. It sits closer to the fabric and gives a refined, understated richness rather than the raised drama of dabka.

I push tilla on grooms who want elegance over weight: a tilla-worked collar and buttons on a self-coloured jamawar, or fine tilla jaali on an off-white sherwani for the walima. It photographs beautifully and never looks gaudy. For a dulha who finds heavy zardozi too much, tilla is the quiet luxury answer.

## Hand versus machine: where the truth shows

This is the question I get every single day, so here it is plainly.

| Feature | Hand work (adda) | Machine work |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Depth and texture | Raised, layered, irregular in a good way | Flatter, uniform |
| Materials possible | Dabka, stones, kundan, real beads | Mostly thread and sequins |
| Durability in dry cleaning | High when done well | Threads can pull or flatten |
| Time per sherwani | 1 to 4 weeks | 1 to 3 days |
| Cost | High | Low to moderate |

Machine embroidery has improved enormously and good machine work is not shameful — for a mehndi outfit or a budget barat, it is sensible. But dabka and stones cannot be done by machine. Anything genuinely raised and stone-set is hand work, full stop. If a shop quotes you "full hand dabka" at a machine price, something is wrong with the dabka, the labour, or the truth.

## Where to place the embroidery

Placement is taste, and most over-embroidered sherwanis fail here. My standing advice:

- **Collar and front placket** — the non-negotiable focus. The camera and the guests look at your chest and face.
- **Cuffs** — a clean band of work that shows when you raise your hands for the rings or the dua.
- **Hemline and side vents** — optional; adds richness for the barat without crowding.
- **Buttons** — worked or kundan buttons lift even a plain sherwani.

Leave the back and the body largely clean. A worked collar against a calm body reads as expensive. Work everywhere reads as a costume.

## What it costs, honestly

In Rawalpindi and Islamabad pricing as of this season: light tilla or fine hand work on the collar and placket runs roughly PKR 60,000 to 90,000 over the base stitching. Mid-weight zardozi with dabka and some stones sits around PKR 120,000 to 200,000. A fully loaded heavy barat sherwani with dense dabka-zardozi and kundan crosses PKR 250,000 easily.
