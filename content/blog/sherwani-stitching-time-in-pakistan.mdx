"Kitne din lagenge?" is genuinely the first thing most grooms ask, before they have even chosen a colour. It is the right question, because your wedding date does not move. After stitching groom wear in Saddar since 1999, here is exactly how long it takes and, more usefully, what makes it take longer.

## The real number

For a made-to-measure sherwani with standard handwork, plan on **10 to 15 working days**. Not calendar days, working days, because karigars rest and embroidery does not move on a closed day. With heavy embroidery or during peak season, three to four weeks is realistic and honest.

Anyone promising you a fully hand-worked barat sherwani in two days is either using machine embroidery and calling it handwork, or skipping the fittings. Both show up later.

## Where those days actually go

A sherwani is built in stages, and each one takes its own time. Here is roughly how a normal 12 working day job breaks down in our workshop.

| Stage | Working days | What is happening |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Cutting and base | 2 to 3 | Panels cut to your naap, canvas chest, lining assembled |
| Embroidery | 5 to 8 | Hand dabka, tilla or zardozi on collar, placket, cuffs |
| First fitting and adjustment | 1 to 2 | Chest, shoulder and length corrected on your body |
| Final finishing | 2 to 3 | Buttons, lining close, second fitting, pressing |

The embroidery is the long pole, almost always. A lightly worked sherwani moves faster. A fully encrusted one can sit on a karigar's frame for two weeks on its own.

## What lengthens the lead time

If your job is running long, it is usually one of these:

- **Embroidery density.** This is the big one. Scattered motifs are quick. A solid zardozi collar and placket are slow, skilled work that cannot be rushed without looking rushed. If you want to understand the handwork driving both time and cost, our [sherwani price guide](/blog/sherwani-price-in-pakistan) explains it.
- **Fabric sourcing.** If you want a specific imported velvet or a banarsi we do not stock, ordering it adds days before stitching even begins. Decide your fabric early using our [jamawar, velvet and banarsi guide](/blog/jamawar-velvet-banarsi-fabric-guide).
- **Fittings that reveal big changes.** If the first fitting shows the chest needs real rebalancing, that adds a cycle. Better that than a sherwani that does not sit right.
- **Indecision.** Changing the colour or design mid-stitch can reset days of work. Commit once and let the karigars work.

## Peak season changes everything

The 10 to 15 day figure assumes a workshop with room. From October to February, it does not have room. Every karigar in Saddar, Raja Bazaar and Commercial Market is loaded with barat and walima orders. We call it Decemberistan, and the backlog alone can add a week to your timeline before a single stitch of yours is done.

So the same sherwani that takes 12 working days in May might realistically take three weeks in December, not because the work is slower but because there is a queue ahead of you. This is why we keep telling grooms to book early. Our note on [how early to order your wedding sherwani](/blog/how-early-to-order-your-wedding-sherwani) covers the season calendar in detail.

## The fittings are not optional

Some grooms ask if they can skip fittings to save time. You can, but you should not. The fittings are where a made-to-measure sherwani earns its name. We adjust the shoulder line, chest balance and length on your actual body, because no naap on paper catches everything.

- **First fitting** happens after the base is built and embroidery is roughed in. This is where the big corrections happen.
- **Second fitting** is a final check before finishing, catching the small things.

Plan to be physically available in Rawalpindi for at least one fitting. If you live in Islamabad or are travelling, tell us up front so we can schedule around it. A sherwani fitted by photo over WhatsApp is a compromise, and we will say so.

## So when should you start?

Work backwards. Take the 10 to 15 working day figure, add buffer for peak season, add fitting visits, and you land at the four to six week lead time we always recommend, or six to eight in winter. If your date is closer than that, do not despair, just come in quickly so we can scope what is genuinely possible.

One mild contrarian point: a slightly simpler design delivered calmly with proper fittings beats an over-ambitious one rushed in half the time. If you are late, we would rather lighten the embroidery and nail the fit than cram dense zardozi into a deadline it cannot meet.
