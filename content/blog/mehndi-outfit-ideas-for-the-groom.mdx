The mehndi is the fun one. There is dholki, there is dancing, the lights are warm, and nobody is being solemn. This is the function where a groom should actually relax about his clothes — and yet every season we get grooms trying to wear something heavy and stiff to a night of jumping around to the dhol. Do not be that man, sweating through a velvet coat while everyone else is twirling. The mehndi wants colour and it wants comfort.

Here is how we dress a dulha for his mehndi.

## Comfort is the whole brief

A mehndi runs for hours. You will be sitting cross-legged on a low stage, then pulled up to dance, then back down, then up again for the rasm. Your outfit has to survive all of that and still look good in the photos at midnight.

- **Choose breathable fabric.** Fine cotton, lawn, khaddar, or a light raw silk. Skip anything lined and heavy.
- **Cut it loose enough to move.** A mehndi kurta should have room through the shoulders and arms. This is not the night for a tight European fit.
- **Kurta or kurta-shalwar both work.** A kurta with chooridar reads a little dressier; a kurta-shalwar is the most relaxed and the most traditional for a desi mehndi.

If you take one thing from this post: you cannot dance freely in a stiff coat, and the mehndi is for dancing.

## Lean into colour

The barat is restrained and the nikah is soft, so the mehndi is your chance to actually enjoy colour. We push grooms toward it every time.

- **Yellow and mustard** — the classic mehndi tones, especially when the family theme is built around marigold and gota.
- **Deep green and bottle green** — flattering on almost every skin tone and a little more grown-up than yellow.
- **White and ivory** — clean, cool, and striking when everyone around you is in colour.
- **Dusty pastels** — sage, blush, and powder blue have become popular with younger grooms and photograph softly.

A printed or self-tone kurta with light gota at the collar and cuffs hits the sweet spot. You want detail, not weight.

## Keep the work light

This is the single most common mehndi mistake we correct. Grooms ask for heavy embroidery on a mehndi kurta, and it is the wrong move on two counts — it makes the kurta hot, and it competes with the barat sherwani that should be your most embellished look. Save the dabka and zardozi for the barat. For the mehndi:

- Light gota kinari at the collar, placket, and cuffs.
- Simple thread work or a printed fabric.
- A self-tone or contrast collar for structure.

Light work also keeps the qeemat sensible. A good mehndi kurta should not cost you a fortune.

## Accessories make the look

This is where a plain kurta becomes a groom's mehndi look. The clothes are simple, so the finishing does the talking.

- **A stole or dupatta** in a contrasting colour, draped over one shoulder.
- **Khussa or kolhapuri** in a bright tone — the mehndi allows playfulness here.
- **Fresh flower haar or kalgi** if the family is doing the gana-bandhi and phool rasm.
- **A simple brooch** to pin the stole, or a beaded mala.

Coordinate with the bride's mehndi outfit — complement her colour, do not match it exactly. Share a photo between you beforehand so neither of you clashes on stage.

## A quick reference

| Element | Mehndi choice |
| --- | --- |
| Garment | Kurta or kurta-shalwar |
| Fabric | Cotton, lawn, khaddar, light raw silk |
| Colour | Yellow, mustard, green, white, pastels |
| Work | Light gota and thread only |
| Finish | Stole, bright khussa, flowers or kalgi |
