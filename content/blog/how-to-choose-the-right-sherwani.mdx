Grooms often arrive at our Saddar shop having already decided everything from a phone screen, then feel stuck when the real fabric in real light does not match the picture in their head. Choosing a sherwani is not about finding one beautiful piece. It is about making six smaller decisions in the right order. After twenty-five years of guiding dulhas through this, here is the framework we actually use at the counter.

## Step 1: Start with the occasion, not the sherwani

Before colour, before fabric, ask what the sherwani is for. A barat sherwani, a walima look and a mehndi outfit are not the same brief.

- **Barat.** The grandest piece, usually the heaviest handwork, classic colours.
- **Walima.** Often sharper and more refined, sometimes a prince coat instead of a sherwani.
- **Mehndi.** Lighter, more playful, brighter colours are welcome here.

If you are dressing for more than one function, decide which one gets the showpiece and which can be lighter. Our [barat, walima and mehndi dress guide](/blog/barat-walima-mehndi-groom-dress-guide) maps the whole sequence so you do not over-invest in the wrong event.

## Step 2: Choose the colour around your skin and the day

Colour is where grooms make the most emotional, least practical choices. A shade that looks regal on a mannequin can wash you out in person. A few honest rules:

- **Match the undertone to your skin.** Warmer skins carry gold, ivory and rich greens beautifully. Cooler skins suit deep maroon, navy and charcoal.
- **Keep barat classic.** Ivory, gold, maroon and deep green photograph well and do not date.
- **Coordinate with the bride, do not copy.** A complementary tone beside her dress looks intentional. The exact same colour looks like a uniform.
- **Save the bold colour for mehndi** if you want to experiment.

If you want a sense of what is current without chasing every trend, our notes on [groom-wear trends for 2026](/blog/groom-wear-trends-2026) and [the best sherwani colours for 2026](/blog/best-sherwani-colours-2026) are a useful sanity check.

## Step 3: Pick the fabric for the function and the weather

Fabric decides how the sherwani sits, how it photographs, and how comfortable you are through a long function. The three that matter most:

| Fabric | Character | Best for |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Jamawar | Structured, traditional, woven pattern | Barat, formal grandeur |
| Velvet | Rich, heavy, deep colour | Winter weddings, evening functions |
| Banarsi | Lustrous, ornate weave | Statement pieces, classic looks |

A heavy velvet at a summer barat will punish you by the second hour. A light jamawar at a December walima may not feel grand enough. Match the cloth to the season as much as the style. Our full [jamawar, velvet and banarsi fabric guide](/blog/jamawar-velvet-banarsi-fabric-guide) goes deeper on each.

## Step 4: Get the fit right, because nothing else matters without it

This is the step grooms underrate and regret most. The most beautiful fabric and the densest zardozi look cheap on a sherwani that does not fit the shoulders. Fit is the whole game.

The areas that make or break it are the shoulder line, the chest balance, and the collar sitting flat against the neck. These are exactly what a camera catches at the barat, and exactly what readymade gets wrong. This is why we push made-to-measure for the dulha himself, as explained in our [made-to-measure versus readymade comparison](/blog/made-to-measure-vs-readymade-sherwani). To understand what we measure and why, see our [sherwani measurement guide](/blog/how-to-take-sherwani-measurements) or the practical [measurement guide](/measurement-guide) on our site.

## Step 5: Set a budget and spend it in the right order

Decide your number before you fall in love with a piece above it. Then spend it in this priority:

1. **Construction and fit first.** A proper canvas chest and a true fit.
2. **Handwork second.** Genuine hand dabka, tilla or zardozi, not machine work passed off as hand.
3. **Fabric third.** A good mid-grade cloth that fits well beats a luxury cloth that hangs badly.
4. **Accessories last, but do not skip them.** Shoes, kulla and buttons that match the level of the sherwani.

For honest figures by tier, our [sherwani price guide](/blog/sherwani-price-in-pakistan) shows what each band of budget actually buys. One mild contrarian point: do not blow the whole budget on embroidery and then cheap out on shoes and a flimsy kulla. It undoes the whole look.

## Step 6: Respect the timeline

The best decision in the world is useless if you order too late to execute it well. A made-to-measure sherwani with proper handwork needs 10 to 15 working days, longer in peak Decemberistan. So work backwards from your function and give yourself four to six weeks, or six to eight in winter. Our pieces on [how early to order](/blog/how-early-to-order-your-wedding-sherwani) and [stitching time in Pakistan](/blog/sherwani-stitching-time-in-pakistan) lay out the calendar.

## Putting it together

Run the six steps in order: occasion, colour, fabric, fit, budget, timeline. Each one narrows the field so that by the time you are standing in front of the fabric, the choice is almost made for you. Grooms who follow this arrive calm and leave confident. Grooms who start by chasing one pretty picture usually circle back to the basics anyway, having wasted a few weeks.
