Grooms forget about their feet until the joota chupai, and then suddenly the whole family is staring at the shoes. I have seen a beautiful barat outfit let down by cheap plastic-soled khussa, and I have seen a simple sherwani made sharp by one good pair of shoes. Footwear is the last decision most dulhas make and it deserves more thought than it gets. Here is how we steer grooms in Saddar on the three real choices.

## Khussa: the traditional default

The khussa is the most traditional groom shoe and, for most barat sherwanis, the most complete-looking. Its flat, pointed shape and the option of zari, tilla, or dabka work on the upper means it carries the embroidery theme of the sherwani right down to the feet. A worked khussa under a heavily embroidered maroon or green velvet sherwani looks intentional and finished in a way no Western shoe quite matches.

Khussa ranges widely in quality and price. In Rawalpindi and Islamabad, a basic leather khussa runs roughly PKR 3,000 to 6,000, a good embroidered pair sits around PKR 8,000 to 18,000, and heavily worked bespoke khussa can climb well past PKR 25,000. Spend on the leather and the sole, not only the embroidery — a beautiful upper on a stiff cardboard sole will punish you by the second hour.

## Peshawari chappal: relaxed but smart

The Peshawari chappal — the open, leather-strapped sandal — is the comfortable middle ground. It is more relaxed than a khussa, reads as smart-casual, and suits a mehndi, a daytime barat, or a groom who simply wants to be able to walk and breathe. Good Peshawari chappal in tan, dark brown, or black leather pairs well with lighter sherwanis and waistcoat looks. It is the choice I recommend to grooms who tell me, honestly, that they will not survive a full day in closed shoes.

## Oxford: the modern, Western-leaning choice

For a prince coat or a sleeker, modern sherwani, a polished Oxford or Derby is sharp and correct. It pulls the look slightly Western and pairs naturally with the more tailored, suit-adjacent silhouette of a prince coat — which we compare against the sherwani in our [sherwani versus prince coat](/blog/sherwani-vs-prince-coat) guide. A dark brown or black Oxford in genuine leather, well polished, looks expensive and clean. Avoid square toes and heavy chunky soles; keep the shape elegant.

## Matching the shoe to the outfit

Here is the quick reference I give grooms during a fitting.

| Outfit | Best shoe | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Heavy barat sherwani | Worked khussa | Carries the embroidery, most traditional |
| Walima or lighter sherwani | Plain khussa or Oxford | Understated, refined |
| Prince coat or modern look | Dark Oxford | Sharp, tailored, Western-leaning |
| Mehndi or daytime | Peshawari chappal | Relaxed, comfortable, smart |

## Colour pairing

The reliable rule: keep the shoe deep and let it sit quietly below the outfit. Dark tan, deep maroon, bottle green, black, and antique gold all work. Match the metal tone of any embroidery on the shoe to the metal tone of your sherwani work — gold khussa with gold dabka, antique with antique. Off-white or cream khussa can work for a walima, but it shows scuffs and dirt fast over a long event, so only choose it if you can keep them clean.

Do not try to match the shoe exactly to the sherwani colour. Coordinate, do not duplicate. A green velvet sherwani with a slightly deeper green or a dark tan khussa looks richer than a flat matching green head to toe. The same coordinate-don't-match logic applies to the pagri, which we cover in [pagri and kulla styles](/blog/pagri-and-kulla-styles).

## Comfort for a long day

This is the part grooms ignore and then regret. A barat plus photos plus walima is many hours on your feet, much of it standing. A few non-negotiables:

1. **Break them in.** Wear new shoes around the house for at least a week so the leather softens and the pressure points reveal themselves before the wedding, not during it.
2. **Add cushioning.** A thin gel or foam insole turns a flat khussa from punishing to bearable. Nobody sees it.
3. **Mind the sole.** A leather or rubber sole with some give beats stiff card. Ask before you buy.
4. **Keep a backup.** A comfortable second pair near the stage for the long sitting stretches is not cheating — it is sense.

## A final word

The shoe is the foundation of the whole look, literally. It does not need to shout, but it must be good quality, broken in, and coordinated with the outfit. A worked khussa for the traditional barat, a Peshawari chappal when comfort leads, an Oxford for the modern prince coat — pick by the outfit and the function, not by what looks flashiest in the shop.
