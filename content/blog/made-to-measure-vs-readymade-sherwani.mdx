Every September our shop fills up with grooms holding a phone, showing a readymade sherwani they saw online for a tempting qeemat, asking whether they should just buy that or get it stitched. It is the right thing to ask before spending the money. After twenty-five years of cutting groom wear in Saddar, here is the honest comparison, not the sales pitch.

## What each one actually means

**Readymade** means the sherwani is cut to a standard size chart, hangs on a rail, and you pick the closest size. A tailor can nip the waist or shorten the sleeve, but the bones of the garment are already set.

**Made-to-measure** means we take your naap, cut the panels to your body, and adjust through two or three fittings before the handwork is finalised. It is your sherwani from the first cut.

There is a third thing, full bespoke, where a pattern is drafted from scratch with multiple toiles. Honestly, for groom wear in Pakistan, made-to-measure covers what almost every dulha needs without the bespoke price.

## Fit: where the real difference lives

This is the whole argument, so let me be blunt. The hardest areas to fit on any sherwani are the shoulders, the chest balance, and the collar sitting flat against the neck. A readymade piece is cut for an average body. If your shoulders are sloped, your chest is fuller, or one shoulder sits lower than the other, which is true for most men, a readymade will pull, gape at the collar, or bunch at the back.

Those are exactly the spots a camera catches at the barat. A tailor can let out a waist seam, but he cannot rebuild a shoulder line that was cut wrong without practically remaking the front. With made-to-measure we set the balance from the start, so the collar hugs and the chest sits clean.

## Cost: the gap is smaller than you think

Grooms assume made-to-measure means double the qeemat. It does not. Here is roughly how they compare:

| Factor | Readymade | Made-to-Measure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Base price | Lower | Ten to thirty percent more |
| Fittings included | Usually none | Two to three |
| Fabric choice | Fixed to what is on the rail | Your pick of jamawar, velvet, banarsi |
| Alteration cost later | Often extra | Built into the process |
| Resale or reuse value | Lower | Holds value, fits only you |

On heavily embroidered designs the price gap shrinks, because the handwork dominates the cost on both. So if you are already spending on dense dabka or zardozi, paying a little more for a true fit is an easy decision. ## Lead time: the one honest advantage of readymade

This is where readymade genuinely wins. You can walk out with it today. Made-to-measure needs time, usually 10 to 15 working days for us, longer in peak Decemberistan when every karigar in Saddar is buried in work.

So if your shaadi is in nine days and you left it late, readymade plus a rushed alteration may be your only realistic option. We will tell you that honestly rather than promise a stitching miracle and disappoint you. ## When readymade actually makes sense

I am not against readymade. It is the right call when:

- The function is minor, like a cousin's mehndi where you are a guest, not the dulha.
- Your timeline collapsed and there is no room to stitch.
- Your body sits close to a standard size and you want to keep the budget tight.
- You want to test a colour or style before committing to a stitched piece.

## When you should commit to made-to-measure

And it is worth the extra when:

1. **It is your own barat or walima.** This is the day. Get it cut for you.
2. **Your body is not a standard size.** Most men's are not.
3. **You are spending on serious handwork.** Do not put expensive embroidery on a poorly fitting base.
4. **You care about the photos and the feeling of wearing it.** A sherwani that fits changes how you carry yourself walking in.

One mild contrarian take: a beautifully made readymade in a great fabric will still lose to a modest made-to-measure that actually fits your shoulders. Fabric impresses on the rail. Fit impresses in person.

## Our honest recommendation

For the dulha himself, made-to-measure, every time. For supporting roles, family functions, or a tight deadline, a good readymade is sensible and nothing to be ashamed of. The mistake we see is grooms buying readymade for their own barat to save a little, then spending the day tugging at a collar that will not sit.
