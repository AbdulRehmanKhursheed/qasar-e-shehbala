People walk into our shop on Saddar Road and the second question, right after "kitne din lagenge", is almost always "achi sherwani ki qeemat kya hai?" It is a fair question and a hard one to answer in one number, because two sherwanis hanging side by side in the same colour can differ by a lakh or more. After stitching groom wear since 1999, let me explain what you are actually paying for, in plain rupees.

## The honest price ranges for 2026

Here is how we see the market today, for made-to-measure work, not readymade rails. These are stitched-and-delivered figures including fabric and standard handwork.

| Tier | Price range (PKR) | What you get |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Entry | 25,000 to 45,000 | Local suiting or light jamawar, mostly machine embroidery, basic lining |
| Mid | 45,000 to 90,000 | Good jamawar or banarsi, mixed hand and machine work, proper canvas front |
| Premium | 90,000 to 180,000 | Velvet or fine jamawar, real dabka and tilla by hand, full hand finishing |
| Heavy bridal | 180,000 and above | Dense zardozi, imported velvet, hours of karigar handwork on collar, placket and cuffs |

Prices in Islamabad showrooms on Bank Road and in some of the bigger Commercial Market setups run higher than ours for the same handwork, mostly because of rent and brand markup. We have always kept our qeemat tied to the actual labour, not the address.

## Handwork is the real cost, not the cloth

This is the part most grooms get wrong. They obsess over the fabric and ignore the embroidery. But a metre of good jamawar might cost a few thousand rupees, while the dabka and zardozi on a single collar can take a karigar three full days. Labour is the expensive thing.

When you see two sherwanis priced at 60,000 and 140,000 in similar velvet, the difference is almost never the velvet. It is:

- **Density of handwork.** A lightly scattered motif versus a fully encrusted placket and collar.
- **Type of work.** Tilla and simple thread are cheaper. Dabka, naqshi and full zardozi are slow and skilled.
- **Who did it.** A senior karigar charges more because his work sits flat and even. Cheap handwork puckers within a season.

If you want to understand the cloth side properly before you set a budget, read our [jamawar, velvet and banarsi fabric guide](/blog/jamawar-velvet-banarsi-fabric-guide). It will stop a shopkeeper from upselling you fabric you do not need.

## What else quietly adds to the bill

Beyond fabric and embroidery, a few things move the final qeemat:

1. **Canvas and inner construction.** A proper floating canvas chest holds shape for years. Fusible glue lining is cheaper and bubbles after a couple of dry-cleans.
2. **Buttons and accessories.** Hand-knotted potli buttons, kora work and a matching dupatta or kulla add up.
3. **Lining quality.** Pure viscose or silk lining breathes. Cheap polyester lining is why some grooms sweat through their own barat.
4. **Fittings.** We build two to three fittings into our process. Shops that skip fittings are cheaper for a reason.

## Red flags of cheap work

Some warning signs we tell every groom to watch for:

- A price that sounds too good for "full hand zardozi" almost always means machine embroidery passed off as handwork. Flip the panel over. Hand dabka has slightly irregular, raised backs. Machine work is flat and perfectly repeating.
- No canvas in the chest. Pinch the front panel between two fingers. If it feels like a single thin sheet of cloth, there is no structure.
- They cannot give you a clear fitting schedule. Good stitching is a process, not a one-day handover.
- Heavy embroidery but a suspiciously light garment. Real metallic dabka has weight.

## So what should you actually budget?

For most grooms doing a proper barat or walima, the honest sweet spot is **PKR 70,000 to 130,000**. That buys good fabric, genuine hand embroidery that will not fall apart, and construction that holds its shape. Below 45,000 you are compromising somewhere, usually on the handwork or the lining. Above 180,000 you are paying for very dense zardozi, which is wonderful but not necessary for everyone.

If you are still deciding between styles before settling the budget, our note on [sherwani versus prince coat](/blog/sherwani-vs-prince-coat) is worth ten minutes, because a prince coat with a separate waistcoat often costs less than a heavily worked sherwani and suits some grooms better.

One mild contrarian opinion, since you asked: do not spend your whole budget on the sherwani and then rent cheap shoes and a plastic-feeling kulla. A 120,000 rupee sherwani with shabby accessories looks worse than an 80,000 one finished properly. Balance the spend.

## Where the money is well spent

If we had to rank where your rupees do the most work, it would be construction first, then handwork, then fabric, then accessories. A modestly embroidered sherwani in mid-grade jamawar with excellent construction and fit will photograph better and last longer than an over-worked piece that hangs badly off the shoulders. Fit is free in the sense that we do not charge extra for it, but it is what people actually notice in the barat photos.
