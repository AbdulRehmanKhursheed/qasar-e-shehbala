Most of the grooms we stitch for walk into the shop in Saddar. But a growing number order from Lahore, Karachi, and abroad — a groom in Dubai who wants a Rawalpindi sherwani for his barat back home. For them, getting the **naap** right at home is everything.

Take your time with this. A made-to-measure sherwani is cut to these numbers, and the fabric is cut once. Here is exactly how we ask grooms to measure themselves.

## Before you start

You need:

- A **soft measuring tape** (the cloth kind, not a steel builder's tape).
- A **helper** — measuring yourself accurately is nearly impossible. Ask a family member.
- **Fitted clothes** — a kurta or a shirt, not a thick jacket or a baggy shalwar.
- Stand straight, relax your shoulders, and breathe normally. Do not puff your chest out or hold your stomach in. We are measuring the real you.

Write each number in **inches**, and label it clearly.

## The seven measurements

### 1. Chest (Seena)

Wrap the tape around the fullest part of your chest, under the arms, keeping it level all the way around. Keep two fingers of slack — do not pull it tight.

### 2. Waist (Kamar)

Measure around your natural waistline, usually just above the navel. Again, level and relaxed. This is your body waist, not your trouser waist.

### 3. Shoulder (Kandha)

Measure across your back, from the bony edge of one shoulder to the bony edge of the other. Your helper should do this one — it is the hardest to self-measure and one of the most important for how a sherwani sits.

### 4. Sleeve length (Bazu)

From the shoulder edge, down the outside of a slightly bent arm, to where you want the cuff to end at the wrist bone.

### 5. Full length (Lambai)

From the top of the shoulder (at the neck) straight down to where you want the sherwani to end. For most grooms this is mid-thigh to just below the knee. Decide this with your height in mind — see our [sherwani vs prince coat guide](/blog/sherwani-vs-prince-coat) for how length affects shorter builds.

### 6. Neck (Gardan)

Around the base of the neck, where a collar sits, with one finger of ease so the collar is never tight.

### 7. Hip (Koolay)

Around the fullest part of the hips, roughly seven to eight inches below the waist. This keeps the sherwani from pulling across the seat.

## The three mistakes we see most

1. **Measuring over thick clothes.** This adds two inches you do not have. Measure over a thin kurta or shirt.
2. **Pulling the tape tight.** A tight tape gives a tight garment. Keep it snug but never pinching.
3. **Guessing the length.** Stand in front of a mirror and physically mark where you want the hem. Do not guess from a number.

## When in doubt, round up

A tailor can always take a garment in. Letting it out is limited by the seam allowance. If you are unsure between two numbers, give us the larger one and a note that you were unsure — we will keep an allowance and confirm before finishing.

For high-value custom orders, we also recommend a short video call during cutting so we can see how the fabric falls on you. It takes ten minutes and removes almost all the risk.
