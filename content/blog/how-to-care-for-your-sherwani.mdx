The shaadi is over, the photos are in, and the sherwani is lying on a chair where the dulha dropped it at 3am. What happens in the next few days decides whether that piece is a keepsake you hand down or a sad, crushed thing you find tarnished in two years. After twenty-five years of making these in Saddar, I can tell you the makhmal and the dabka do not forgive neglect. Here is exactly how we tell our grooms to look after them.

## First, let it breathe

Do not fold a worn sherwani away immediately. After a long day it has absorbed sweat, attar, and humidity, and sealing that in is how stains set and metal thread tarnishes. Hang it on a wide padded hanger in an open, dry room for a full day or two. Let the fabric release moisture and the creases relax on their own. This single habit prevents most of the damage I see.

While it airs, check it over. Note any food spots, any pulled threads, any loose stones. Catching a snagged dabka coil now, before it unravels a whole row, saves an expensive repair later.

## Cleaning: less is more

The biggest mistake grooms make is over-cleaning. Every dry clean stresses the embroidery — the solvents and the pressing flatten raised dabka and can tarnish tilla. So clean only when you genuinely need to.

- **Light marks only:** spot-clean by hand. Dab, never rub, with a barely damp cloth on plain fabric areas. Keep all moisture away from the embroidery.
- **Visible stains or odour:** take it to a dry cleaner who handles bridal and embroidered wear, not the corner shop. Tell them clearly it is hand zardozi and ask them to hand-finish, not machine-press.
- **Never:** machine wash, soak, or hand wash a structured sherwani. The canvas inside the chest will warp and never sit right again.

If you own velvet, the rules are stricter still — our [velvet winter guide](/blog/velvet-sherwani-winter-guide) covers steaming and pile care, because heat on velvet is permanent damage.

## Ironing and steaming

Never put a hot iron directly on embroidery or velvet. For plain cotton or silk areas, iron on low with a cotton cloth between the iron and the fabric. For everything embroidered, steam is your friend — a hand steamer held a few inches away relaxes creases without crushing the work. Always steam from the reverse where you can, and let the piece dry fully before storing.

## Storing it properly

How you store the sherwani matters more than how you clean it, because it spends years in storage and only hours on your back.

| Piece type | Best storage | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Light embroidery | Hang on a wide padded hanger | Keeps the shape, low weight stress |
| Heavy zardozi or velvet | Lay flat or roll in a box | Hanging stretches shoulders under the weight |
| Buttons and stones | Wrap separately in tissue | Prevents snagging the fabric |

The cover is critical. Use breathable cotton or muslin, never a sealed plastic bag — plastic traps moisture and that is what tarnishes your gold and silver thread and breeds mildew. Lay a sheet of soft muslin or acid-free butter paper over the embroidery before folding so the metal does not rub against itself. Add a few naphthalene balls or cloves nearby, kept away from direct contact with the cloth, to keep insects off the silk and wool.

Keep the whole thing in a dry, dark place. Sunlight fades deep maroons and greens over time, and damp is the enemy of everything metallic.

## Protecting the embroidery specifically

The dabka, zardozi, and tilla are the most valuable and most fragile part. A few habits protect them:

1. Keep perfume and attar away from the worked areas — alcohol tarnishes metal thread fast. Spray your scent before you dress.
2. Take the sherwani off carefully; do not yank it over jewellery or watches that can catch a coil.
3. If a stone or coil comes loose, bag it and bring it in. We can re-set it cleanly far more easily than rebuilding a damaged section.

## Refreshing it for a second wear

A good sherwani is built to be worn again — for a brother's barat, a friend's walima, an anniversary shoot. To bring it back to life: air it for two days, steam out the storage creases, check and re-secure any loose buttons or stones, and give the embroidery a gentle going-over with a soft dry brush to lift the shine. If the fit has changed, a small alteration at the waist or sleeve is usually possible; bring it to us with the original [measurements](/measurement-guide) and we will assess it.

A well-kept piece can be restyled too — a new pagri and a different khussa change the whole feel without a new sherwani. Our [accessories guide](/blog/groom-accessories-guide) has ideas for that.
