Velvet is the most dramatic cloth we cut. Deep, soft, light-absorbing — a bottle-green or maroon velvet sherwani under the indoor lighting of a Pindi marquee is unforgettable. But it is also the fabric grooms get most wrong, usually by wearing it in the wrong month or buying it cheap. Let me give you the honest version after a quarter-century of dressing dulhas through the cold-weather rush.

## Why velvet owns winter

Our heavy wedding season runs October through February, and that is exactly velvet's home ground. The pile traps warmth, so a velvet sherwani is comfortable on a cold barat evening when a thin silk would leave the dulha shivering between photos. More than warmth, though, it is the look. Velvet does not reflect light like jamawar or banarsi; it absorbs it, then releases a deep glow at the edges where the pile shifts. That depth is why every embroidery thread laid on velvet looks twice as rich. Dabka and zardozi on velvet is the most expensive-looking combination we make.

Quality matters more here than on any other fabric. Cheap velvet looks cheap instantly — thin, shiny in a plasticky way, and visible from across the room. Good velvet has a dense, short pile, a matte depth, and weight in the hand. When you shop, do not judge velvet by a phone photo; rub it between your fingers.

## The colours that work

Some velvet colours carry a barat and some fight the lighting. After thousands of fittings, this is where I land:

| Colour | Best for | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Maroon | Barat | The classic. Warm, regal, photographs beautifully. |
| Bottle green | Barat, walima | Modern favourite, stunning with antique-gold work. |
| Midnight navy | Barat, walima | Safer than black, still dramatic, very flattering. |
| Black | Barat | Sharp but needs strong embroidery or it reads flat. |
| Wine and aubergine | Mehndi, sangeet | Rich without being the full barat statement. |

Off-white velvet exists but I rarely recommend it — the pile marks and shows handling far too easily for a long event.

## Weight and comfort

Velvet is heavy, and when you load it with zardozi it becomes heavier still. A fully worked velvet barat sherwani can carry real weight on the shoulders. For most grooms this is fine for a few hours, but if you are slight in build or the event runs long, tell your tailor — we can use a lighter cotton-backed velvet and keep the embroidery to the collar, placket, and cuffs rather than the full body. The naap and the inner construction matter here; a well-cut canvas inside the chest carries the weight so your shoulders do not.

Lining is not optional with velvet. A smooth silk or cotton lining stops the pile dragging on your kurta and lets the sherwani slide on and off cleanly during a long shaadi day.

## When to avoid velvet

I turn grooms away from velvet more often than people expect, and it is always about the season and the time of day.

- **A summer wedding (April to September).** Velvet in heat is miserable. You will sweat through your photos. Choose jamawar or a wool-silk blend instead.
- **A daytime walima in a hot hall.** Even in mild weather, indoor crowds and lighting make velvet warm.
- **A destination or outdoor barat in strong sun.** The pile reads almost black and the heat is punishing.

If your event falls outside the cold months, do not force it. ## Caring for it after

Velvet rewards careful handling. The rules are simple and non-negotiable:

1. **Never iron the face.** Heat crushes the pile permanently. Steam from the reverse, or have it professionally steamed.
2. **Always dry clean.** Tell the cleaner it is embroidered velvet so they protect the dabka.
3. **Hang it wide and padded.** A thin wire hanger leaves shoulder dents in velvet that never fully recover.
4. **Keep it dry.** Damp flattens the pile and tarnishes metallic thread. A breathable cloth cover, never plastic.

We have a full routine in our [sherwani care guide](/blog/how-to-care-for-your-sherwani) if you plan to keep the piece for years, which with good velvet you absolutely can.

## A word on styling

Velvet is bold, so let the rest stay calm. A simple kulla and pagri in a tone that picks up the embroidery, a clean khussa, and minimal jewellery. The fabric is already doing the talking.
